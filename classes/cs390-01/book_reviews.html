Introduction to Robotics — Book Reviews

Four books are under consideration for the introduction to robotics course:

  • B — Bekey, George A. (2005). Autonomous Robots: From Biological Inspiration to Implementation and Control. The MIT Press.
  • SN — Siegwart, Roland & Nourbakhsh, Illah R. (2004). Introduction to Autonomous Mobile Robotics. The MIT Press.
  • N — Nehmzow, Ulrich (2003). Mobile Robotics: A Practical Introduction (2nd ed.). Springer.
  • DJ — Dudek, Gregory & Jenkin, Michael (2000). Computational Principles of Mobile Robotics. Cambridge University Press.
  1. Introduction, What Is Robotics?, Motivation

    What I was looking for was essentially two high-level stories. One, a story of robotics; who came up with the idea, how things progressed, where we're at today, and where we might be going. Also, I wanted a parallel overview of the state of the art of robotic control solutions; purely teleoperated to functional control to behavior-based to higher levels of autonomy. Something to give a student both an idea of why these problems are interesting and also an overview of the state of the art.

    1. B 1 — Recent examples, control flow diagrams, biological metaphors. An engineering perspective but with some societal understanding.
    2. N 2 — Good coverage of evolution of control systems. Old examples.
    3. SN 1 — Lots of pictures. Very little history.
    4. DJ 1 — Old examples, emphasizes the mobile robotics context.
  2. Locomotion

    Looking for a description of locomotion kinematics and transformations of reference frames. Also the basic types of locomotive actuators and their characteristics.

    1. DJ 2 — Fewer types of wheels, focuses on other environments such as space and submarine. Explanations aren't as deep, but more approachable.
    2. SN 2 — Very comprehensive listing with extensive mathematical details. Very little explanation, really a better reference than a learning text.
    3. B 7, 8, & 9 — Extensive examples of many types including several biomimetic machines such as fish-modeled submarines and brachiating chimp-modeled swingers, also UAV examples. Entire chapter on bipedal locomotion. Second chapter on tri- and greater pedal locomotion. Nothing about wheels.
  3. Kinematics

    Looking for an understanding of how motion of the robots actuators affect the robot's position. Potentially as well a description of how manipulator actions should be controlled.

    1. SN 3 — Extensive description of various wheel types. Nothing on legs or arms.
    2. B 10 — Exclusive description of arms. Less mathematical depth.
  4. Control

    1. DJ 6 — Engineering with more examples and a section on multiple robots.
    2. B 4 — Engineering and mathematical description.
  5. Architectures
    1. B 5
  6. Actuators
    1. B 3.2, 11 — Focused largely on limbs with a mention of motors. Entire chapter on grasping using hands.
    2. N 3.3 — One page giving an overview of types.
  7. Arms and Grasping
    1. B 10, 11
  8. Sensors: Basic (non-visual sensors)
    1. SN 4.1 — Extensive description of sensor types and issues.
    2. N 3.2 — Cursory overview of all sensors.
    3. DJ 3.1-3.8, 3.11 — General description of several sensor with light mathematical details.
    4. B 3.3 — Conceptual overview focused on error issues for each type.
  9. Sensors: Visual Sensors
    1. DJ 4
  10. Sensors: Uncertainty & Intro Feature Extraction
    1. SN 4.2 — Examples of several algorithms with pictures and equations.
    2. DJ 4.4 — More concrete examples, but older and with lighter mathematical detail.
  11. Sensors: Feature Extraction & Sensor Fusion
    1. SN 4.3 — Concrete examples of several different robots and their sensor fusion methods.
    2. DJ 3.9 — Primarily Kalman Filtering.
    3. N 3.5, 6 — Focus on "novelty detection" with light technical details.
  12. Representation and Reasoning
    1. DJ 5.1 - 5.2
  13. Navigation
    1. B 14.1 - 14.3 — Good overview of uncertainty, dead reckoning and mapping. More comprehensible.
    2. SN 6 — Extensive coverage of navigational methods with equations and examples.
    3. N 5 — Focuses heavily on robot, FortyTwo, used throughout book. Good depth on issues covered, but not as much abstraction.
  14. Mapping
    1. DJ 8
  15. Localization
    1. SN 5 — Lots of math, concrete examples.
    2. DJ 7 — Mathematical type approach with theorems and proofs. More conceptual.
  16. Planning
    1. SN 6 — Extensive listing of obstacle avoidance methods. Case studies. Less depth on AI.
    2. DJ 5.3-5.4 — Bug algorithm. Fewer examples and little depth.
  17. Learning
    1. N 4 — Overview of variety of applicable methods from AI. Enough depth to find more information as needed.
    2. B 6 — Focus on neural nets and genetic algorithms. Concrete robotics algorithms focusing on biomimetics.
  18. Multiple Robots
    1. B 12
  19. Humanoids
    1. B 13
  20. Practical Robot Tasks
    1. DJ 9
